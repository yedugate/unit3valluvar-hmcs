Minerals – Protective Nutrients

11th–12th grade • Definition & basics • Classification • Sources & functions • One absorption diagram • Printable

1) Definition & Biochemistry

Minerals are inorganic elements (no carbon framework) required in small amounts for life.

  • Form parts of bones, teeth, enzymes, hemoglobin, hormones, and body fluids.
  • Cannot be synthesized by the body → must be obtained from food.
Inorganic Enzymes & hormones Bones & blood

2) Classification of Minerals

Group Needed in… Examples Main Role (Simple)
Macrominerals >100 mg/day Calcium, Phosphorus, Sodium, Potassium, Magnesium Bone structure, fluid balance, nerve signals
Microminerals (Trace) <100 mg/day Iron, Iodine, Zinc, Copper, Fluoride, Selenium Blood, thyroid, enzymes, immunity
Ultratrace elements Very tiny (µg) Chromium, Molybdenum, Cobalt Specialized enzyme functions

4) RDA (General Values for Adults)

  • Calcium: ~1000 mg/day
  • Iron: ~17–21 mg/day
  • Iodine: ~150 µg/day
  • Zinc: ~10–12 mg/day
  • Sodium: <5 g salt/day (≈ 2 g Na)
  • Potassium: ~3500 mg/day

Values are general adult references; needs vary by age, sex, and physiological state (pregnancy, lactation).

5) Diagram – Absorption of Minerals (Simplified)

Simple flow: release in stomach → absorption in small intestine → transport via blood to target tissues.

✅ Quick Recap for Students

  • Minerals = inorganic nutrients needed in macro (>100 mg/day) or micro (<100 mg/day) amounts.
  • Major: Ca, P, Na, K, Mg; Trace: Fe, I, Zn, F, Cu, Se.
  • Vital roles: Bones, blood, enzymes, hormones, nerve impulses.
  • Deficiency → anemia, goiter, weak bones; Excess → toxicity (e.g., kidney stones, hypertension).
